# 03. Sprint 2: Social & Professional

Bengaluru's social grammar is built on respect and indirect politeness. Mastering these phrases will help you transition from a visitor to a local colleague.

## 1. The Universal Icebreaker
In Bangalore, you don't generally ask "How are you?" as a primary greeting. You ask about food.

*   **Oota aitha? (Had lunch/dinner?):** The #1 Bangalore greeting. It acts as a universal icebreaker.
    *   *Usage:* Always asked after saying "Hello" or "Namaskara".
*   **Chennagiddini (I am fine/good):** The standard positive response to inquiries about your well-being (like *Heggiddira?*).

## 2. Information Gathering (Interrogatives)
Unlike Hindi, which often places question words (*Kya*) at the ends of sentences, Kannada uses specific interrogative roots.

*   **Yenu? (What?):** The universal interrogative for objects and concepts; can also express surprise.
    *   *Usage:* "Idhu **yenu**?" (What is this?)
*   **Yaru? (Who?):** The interrogative used specifically for identifying people.
    *   *Usage:* "Ivaru **yaru**?" (Who is this person?)
*   **Hesar(u) (Name):** Essential for introductions.
    *   *Usage:* "Nimma **hesaru** yenu?" (What is your name? - Respectful)
*   **Ellidira? (Where are you?):** 
    *   *Usage:* "Neevu **ellidira**?" (Where are you right now?)

## 3. Office Logistics and Work Culture
Communicating status updates efficiently.

*   **Kelsa (Work):** 
    *   *Usage:* "Nanage **kelsa** ide" (I have work).
*   **Mugithu (Finished/Over):** 
    *   *Usage:* "Meeting **mugithu**" (Meeting over). "Kelsa **mugithu**" (Work is finished).
*   **Swalpa (A little):** A highly utilized adverb that functions as a polite conversational softener.
    *   *Usage:* "**Swalpa** busy iddini" (I am a little busy).
*   **Nade (Walk/Move/Go):**
    *   *Usage:* "Meeting-ge **nade**" (Let's walk to the meeting).

## 4. Time Management and Scheduling

*   **Ivathu (Today):** 
    *   *Usage:* "**Ivathu** raje ide" (Today is a holiday).
*   **Naale (Tomorrow):** Often used to politely postpone meetings or visits.
    *   *Usage:* "**Naale** sigona" (Let's meet tomorrow).

## 5. Joining Ideas (Conjunctions)
These words are the glue for building more complex, natural-sounding sentences.

*   **Matthe (And / Then):** Used to link similar items in lists or connect parallel ideas.
    *   *Usage:* "Coffee **matthe** tea" (Coffee and tea).
*   **Adre (But):** Essential for introducing contrast.
    *   *Usage:* "Bēku, **adre** illa" (Want, but don't have).
*   **Athava (Or):** Used for presenting options or choices.
    *   *Usage:* "Coffee **athava** tea?" (Coffee or tea?)
*   **Haage (Like that):** 
    *   *Usage:* "**Haage** maadi" (Do it like that).

## 6. The Cultural Goodbye
In Kannada culture, you rarely say a definitive "I am going" (*Naanu hogthini*) when leaving a place, as it is historically considered inauspicious (implying you are leaving and never returning). 

Instead, the culturally accurate and polite way to take leave is to say:

**"Naanu hogi bartini"** (I will go and come) or **"Hogi baruttene"** (Respectful).

*   **Literal Meaning:** "Having gone, I will come back."
*   **Context / Usage:** Use this when leaving a friend's house, wrapping up a conversational exchange, or saying goodbye to colleagues. It signifies respect, optimism, and the implicit promise that the relationship or connection will continue.
